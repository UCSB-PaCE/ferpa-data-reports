UCSB use case

Self-service knowledge assistant

For Anyone answering the same questions from scattered docs

Curated a trusted set of sources and stood up an AI assistant that answers from it with citations and a human gate, instead of fielding the same questions over and over. Teams built a resource concierge for analysts, a compliance rewriter that returns your plan with the right policies and account strings, a sponsored-projects proposal helper, a notes synthesizer so knowledge stops living in one person's head, and a distilled step-by-step guide for a notoriously confusing policy.

NotebookLMGemini GemsLLM Sandbox

How it works

The same pattern, applied to a pile of trusted documents: you curate the sources, an assistant answers from only those with citations, and a person stays at the gate for anything that matters.

  1. 1
    You

    Curate the trusted sources

    Gather only the documents you actually trust to answer from (the current policy, the approved guide, the real account strings) and drop the stale or contradictory ones. The quality of every answer is capped by this set, so a small, clean, current folder beats a big inconsistent one. Keep anything protected to a tool approved for that data level (on UCSB accounts, NotebookLM and Gemini are approved up to P4; LLM Sandbox is the campus-run gateway option).

  2. 2
    AI

    Ground the assistant on those sources

    Load the curated set into the tool (NotebookLM notebook, a Gemini Gem, or a bot on LLM Sandbox) so it answers from your documents instead of its general training. This is the one thing that turns a generic chatbot into a self-service desk for your team: same question asked a hundred times, one grounded answer every time.

    Prompt to paste 
    Write the setup instruction for a grounded assistant (a NotebookLM notebook, a Gemini Gem, or an LLM Sandbox bot) for {the topic, e.g. travel reimbursement}. Tell it: answer only from the attached sources, never from outside knowledge; for every claim, cite the exact source and section it came from in brackets like [Travel Policy, sec. 4]; when the sources do not cover the question, reply "I do not have a source for that" and name who to ask ({the human owner, e.g. the finance team}) instead of guessing; keep the tone {plain and brief, e.g. two or three sentences}. Do not invent account strings, dollar amounts, or dates that are not in the sources.
  3. 3
    You / A person

    Set the rules, then keep the gate

    Test it with the questions your team actually asks and confirm each answer cites a real source you can open. Decide what the assistant may answer on its own (routine "where is the form" lookups) and what still needs a human sign-off (anything that commits money, dates, or policy exceptions). Re-check the citations whenever the source documents change, and keep a person on the answers that carry consequences.
