No code needed

Do it in Google Colab

You can run this whole pipeline without writing code. You work in Google Colab, an AI helper writes each step for you, and you read it and press run. The result is the same report a developer would build. This page walks the whole thing, slowly, in plain language. Brand new to Colab? Take the five-minute primer first.

The shape of it

What you will do #

Three roles do the work, and it helps to keep them straight, because it is what makes this safe to do with real data.

  • An AI helper writes the code. You describe what you want in plain words; it writes the small program. You never type code yourself.
  • You approve every step. Nothing runs until you read it and press the button. You are the person in charge, the whole way through.
  • A model summarizes the free text, once. In a single step, your written comments go to a model that turns them into a short, cited summary. That is the only place your data is sent to a model, and it goes to a gateway your institution controls.
Two different AIs, do not mix them up The helper that writes code (Gemini, built into Colab) is one thing. The model that summarizes your comments (through your organization's gateway) is another. Keep real records out of the code helper's chat; the records belong only in the summarizing step, which runs through the approved gateway.

A short list

What you need #

  • A Google account, to use Colab.
  • A base URL and key for a language-model gateway your organization controls. At UCSB, that is the LLM Sandbox; get one there. Not at UCSB? See what a gateway is and how to get one.
  • Your data, as a spreadsheet or CSV. A synthetic sample is provided so you can practice with no real records.

Step 1

Open the notebook #

The fastest start is the ready-made notebook for this guide; the link is at the bottom. Open it in Colab and it arrives with every step already laid out. If you would rather build it yourself, open a blank notebook at colab.research.google.com and follow along; the AI helper will fill in each cell.

Step 2

Add your key, safely #

Your key is like a password, so it goes in Colab's Secrets panel, not in a cell. Click the key icon on the left edge and add three secrets, turning on "Notebook access" for each:

  • LLM_BASE_URL, your gateway's base URL (it ends in /v1)
  • LLM_API_KEY, your key
  • LLM_MODEL, a model id (for example claude-v4.5-haiku at UCSB)

Step 3

Meet your AI helper #

Colab has a built-in AI assistant (Gemini). It is how a non-coder writes code: you tell it what you want, and it writes the cell. You will see a "Generate" prompt and a way to ask it to fix or change a cell. You stay in charge, you read what it wrote and decide whether to run it.

At UCSB: Gemini in Colab is cleared to P3 Google's Gemini, the Colab helper, is approved for UCSB data up to Protection Level 3 (P3), and to P4 only with prior authorization. You are only asking it to write code, so the safe move is to never paste a real record into it. Confirm your data's level and the current rules on the UCSB AI resources page before you start.

Step 4

Bring in your data #

Click the folder icon on the left to open Files, and upload your spreadsheet for the session. It is removed when you close the notebook, which for protected data is exactly what you want.

Step 5

Shape the data #

This is where the AI helper earns its keep. Real spreadsheets are rarely clean: odd column names, blank rows, inconsistent labels. Tell the helper, in plain words, what you have and what you want. Something like: "Read advising_notes.csv. Rename the advisor column to subject and the notes column to text. Drop empty rows. Show me the first five." It writes the cell; you press run and look at the result.

If the result looks wrong, tell it what is off and it tries again. This back-and-forth is the whole skill, and it is one a smart person picks up in an afternoon.

Step 6

The one AI step: insight #

Now the single step where your data meets a model. The notebook sends just the free-text comments, numbered, to your gateway, and asks for a short summary that cites the comments it used. Names and other identifiers never go; only the text does. The summary comes back grounded in real quotes, so it cannot invent a complaint nobody made.

This is the responsible part, made simple The comments go to the gateway your institution approved, not to a public chatbot, and not to the code helper. Nothing is kept for training. And you still read the summary before it goes anywhere.

The model also costs almost nothing here, and the notebook remembers each answer, so running it again is free. If you want to understand that step in depth, the developer guide shows exactly what is sent.

Step 7

Make the report #

Ask the helper for the charts you want from the numeric columns (a bar chart of scores by question, say), and to lay the charts and the summary into a clean report you can download. Then the most important step, the one no AI does for you: you read the finished report and decide whether to share it. A person approving the result is the rule that keeps the whole thing trustworthy.

An AI wrote the code, a model summarized the text, and you approved what shipped. That division is the point, and you just ran all three.

Where this leaves you

You can do this now #

You did not learn to code. You learned something more useful: how to direct an AI to do the coding, how to keep protected data inside safe walls, and how to stay the person in charge. Point this at your next pile of data and you will move faster the second time. That is the whole idea, more people who can turn their own data into something worth reading.
